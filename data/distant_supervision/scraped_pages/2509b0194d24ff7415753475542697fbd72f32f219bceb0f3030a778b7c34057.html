Laeeth Isharc, Student of collapse and catastrophe
Updated 24w ago

Its June now, and the election is in November. The answer to your question about the prospect of a collapse in the economy for the beginning of any five month period is almost always no, because collapses are very rare. Its an odd question to ask too, because the housing market is pretty firm, the labour market is very close to full employment (with inflation-adjusted wage growth for men year on year close to the levels of 1999, when they were so desperate for people they started to train convicts for new jobs before they even got out of jail), and equity markets arent far off the highs. Commodities have been weak, and the yield curve has flattened (a bit steeper recently) but both have reversed a bit since the most extreme levels earlier in the year.

In general if people are fretting about a pending recession (which they have since October) and the data arent consistent with that then its worth at least suspending judgement. Growth has slowed as China changed gears, and that plus the problems of Europe has something to do with the commodity selloff and flattening yield curve (I wrote about the likelihood of these things back in May 2011, and China switching gears was a core theme in my fund in 2013, but its certainly taken slightly longer than I originally expected to play out).

When one is dealing with markets, one is dealing with wild animals, and anything could happen. I have a friend who has been very right the past couple of years (not so much since February) who expects a European banking collapse and global yields much lower. I dont really see that myself though.

Recessions and growth-slowdowns do happen quite often. If you go to the website of the BER you will see that we used to have a 3.55 year restocking cycle, which attenuated after World War 2, and disappeared in the extended boom that began in the 1980s. So recoveries were often quite short. I wrote some years back, and Soros later agreed with me (I doubt he read my work), that we are back to more of an era of stop-go - in a similar but different pattern to what Britain experienced after WW2. A recession or slowdown is not an economic collapse though - it just feels like one if you are in a sector most affected by it. If you move from a country where it never rains to one where it often rains heavily during a certain season, perhaps it feels like a monsoon. But a monsoon is something quite different (I use the word in the older English sense, not the modern technical one).

Kitchin cycle

As regards the dollar, I was bullish from June 2011 till early this year. I am not bullish any longer, for various reasons.

Furthermore, Republican presidents tend to be bad for the dollar; Democrats tend to be good - I am not sure why this is, and it may or may not reflect whether their respective policies are good for the nation; nonetheless it is an empirical pattern. Since monetary policy is currently very easy, and Trump is likely to spend heavily on infrastructure by borrowing money, and this is a classic policy for currency weakness, if perceptions should reflect growing prospect of a Trump win, then we could see quite sharp dollar depreciation - particularly because the dollar is currently very overbought against emerging-market, commodity-related, and some european currencies - but I would be surprised by a true collapse.

Addendum: you know what? At least a sharp slowdown accompanied by/following equity weakness is possible. The political response to Brexit by UK and EU figures has really not been encouraging, and in spite of all the fearmongering before hand, people arent nearly as worried as I would like them to be to feel confident it will be okay in the short-term. Funding stress and some other things could be pretty bullish the dollar in that scenario.

Schumpeter said about the 1930s - people for the most part stood their ground, but the ground itself was about to give way before them (paraphrasing from memory). And theres a little of that feeling today.
