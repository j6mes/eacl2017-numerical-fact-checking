What's a nice "rejection" letter to send a runner up candidate?

I'm looking for some nice examples of rejection letters to send Top 5 candidates who, obviously, did not get the job offer.

Update; Should it come from the internal recruiter (traditionally their point of contact) or the hiring manager?

if you are working on 30 reqs at any given time, calling them isn't really an option from a time productivity point of view. however i agree with James.
Thank them for their time for attending the interview and that you and your team enjoyed meeting them very much.

Dear Joe,

Thankyou for taking the time to attend the interview on (date). It was a great pleasure for the team and I to meet you.
The competition for this role was high and you were one of the few candidates we selected for final interview.
It was a tough decision to make, your skills and experience in (x area/sector) are very valuable however we will not be taking the process further in this instance. We will be extending an offer to another candidate who had experience in an additional skill(whatever...explain)

We will be recruiting again for similar positions later in the year(or whatever time scale)

The general feedback was really positive and I'd like to keep the door open for future opportunities.

Thanks again for your interest in our company and do keep your eye on our jobs page,

Please feel free to ping me with any questions

(please note i just quickly put this together - but gives you an idea - big them up, thank them for going to the interview and taking time out, tell them general feedback, what everyone thought of that person, be nice then let them know they didn't get through and leave the door open - a rejection letter sucks the above rejection letter will also suck but it makes the candidates feel a lot better.

Taking a different approach to the answer - this is the kind of rejection we should be sending to candidates.

Preparation -

Before we even jump into rejection we need to make sure we have run the job process correctly. Have you responded to everyone who contacted you regarding the role? Have your released all the candidates who you spoke with but who weren't shortlisted? Have you notified those who applied with an irrelevant profile? Those people are just as important as the 5 or 10 who were interviewed for the role. Are we are recruiters consciously doing as much as we could and should to communicate with these people? I know I'm not.

Always start with an email -

Some people prefer phone calls, some prefer emails. No one prefers to be called out of the blue with bad news. They can't prepare, they can't process and few people are ready to discuss.

Send an email to everyone you need to release along the lines of:

â€œI have an update on (role) for you. It looks like this process won't continue. I'd be happy to discuss the details with you. Feel free to call me at (number) between (times). If I'm not avaliable when you call, leave a message and I'll call you back.â€

When the call happens -

Probably 10% will call you right away. 20% will reply with disappointment and a request to be updated on future roles. 20% will try to reach you or set up a call during the next few weeks. 50% won't reply. For those who don't reply, I would plan to call those people who you feel the feedback will value-add to them. The more you give-forward/create value in the people you work with, the more they will recommend and work with you.

When the call happens be radically transparent. I'm increasingly a fan of being professionally clear about why they weren't selected - examples:

You were 10 minutes late without warning. Yes it is a director level role. That makes every impression so much more important.

Or,

The team liked you and they felt you could do the job. One of the other candidates was rated higher by the panel of interviews. Especially in how they presented the strategy for the business.

Other notes -

  1. Time - This approach take time. You will need to rethink your priorities each day.
  2. Future roles - I recommend telling all candidates that this doesn't disqualify them from future roles with the same company and they are welcome to reapply in the future. No matter how silly the mistake, they should always be reconsidered. (Unless it was frankly unethical or illegal).
  3. Line up - many candidates will ask â€œhow do I rankâ€; â€œdid you represent othersâ€; â€œam I the only candidateâ€. I recommend being honest. If they are the only candidate, discussing what that means with them unlocks a lot more you can do with that candidate rather than playing some silly game of cat and mouse.
  4. Attribution - it is vital that any feedback you give is â€œunattributedâ€. Feedback should never be: â€œSam said youâ€¦ and that's why it's a no go.â€ All your feedback should be designed to add value and help them understand what they should do differently next time.
  5. Documentation - beyond the first email and a followup â€œgreat speaking with you just now and looking forward to working with you againâ€ - nothing should be documented by email. Even carefully handled feedback can be forwarded, posted online, and otherwise focused in a way you wouldn't expect.

Though higher standards and a commitment to add value we can make recruitment a more positive, self-growth experience for everyone.

As the others mentioned, calling is always best - the more time someone spends with you and your team the more time and thought you should put into the rejection.

I'm actually a hiring manager - but am often prospecting for candidates myself. Anyone that I bring into the interview process, I like to follow up with personally, since I'm the one who established interest in the first place.

I think the examples given here are both great examples. What I hear from candidate's most often is that they do want some transparency and feedback, obviously that's hard sometimes, but if the feedback will help them improve, it's good to give that to them directly.

Otherwise Ruby and James both mentioned, leave the door open for future opportunities. I've had a couple candidates I've brought back for other roles at a later date.

I think Ruby's answer below is excellent.  I'd just add one thing:

Try and give genuine feedback if you can, because this is incredibly valuable.

If you can offer something real, like:
'You were very good, but another candidate had more experience in B2B, which is important to us'

or

'We thought that your admin and face-to-face experience was great, but we're seeking someone with more experience in telephony sales.'

Basically, if you can give the applicants a genuine reason that they didn't get the job, you'll give them something to work on for the future.

I'm sure pretty everyone can testify that it's frustrating if you don't get a job, but even more so if you don't know why!

And good on you for asking - many businesses don't find the time to care :)
