Product Description

Product Description

Geza von Bolvary directs this silent mystery thriller. After a series of devastating train crashes, the hunt is on for the villainous saboteur (played by American film star Carlyle Blackwell) who is plotting them. The film, which was thought lost for many years, features an early script from Angus MacPhail, who went on to provide the screenplay for the 1949 classic 'Whisky Galore!'.

5.0 out of 5 starsExcellent presentation of fascinating film
By Steve VINE VOICE on 6 Dec. 2009
Format: DVD Verified Purchase
The main item on this DVD is the 1928 British film 'The Wreckers'. It is silent, black and white, and approximately 70 minutes long. There is a fine musical score specially composed for this film, and a couple of brief, but pretty essential, added sound effects.

It's fair to say that the acting and picture quality are not up to today's standards. But it is 81 years old! It is very expensive to restore films frame by frame; the picture is perfectly watchable anyway, and the actual transfer to DVD is fine. (I have seen much more modern films ruined by poor DVD tranfers, with video noise and pixellation).

The story is based on a play of the same name by Arnold Ridley (who played Godfrey in 'Dad's Army'). He also wrote the play 'Ghost Train'. I think Ridley must have liked trains as both these plays are based around them. In The Wreckers, someone is blowing up track causing trains to derail, causing death and destruction. People are understandably frightened, and are using rival bus companies. The railway companies are losing custom, and the hunt is on to find the villain.

The film is not a masterpiece, but it is extremely interesting in a number of ways. Firstly, there is a large amount of location railway filming. The then Southern Railway cooperated with the producers, letting them film at locations such as Waterloo, London Bridge, Chislehurst, and elsewhere, including shots from the footplate and from carriages. There is at least one overhead shot showing multiple railway lines, and a few showing electric multiple units passing on the other line to our steam train. There are some very clear inside shots of signalboxes too. The most famous scene in the film is where a whole train is actually wrecked.Read more ›

4.0 out of 5 starsTrains are superb, drama is punk
By David Shepard on 26 Nov. 2009
Format: DVD Verified Purchase
I purchased and viewed a DVD copy of THE WRECKER from amazon.co.uk. It is distributed by a company that specializes in transport and military films, rather than a distributor of art or nostalgia movies.

The film is a Gainsborough picture of 1928 (and a German co-production) directed by Geza von Bovary, with Carlyle Blackwell and Benita Hume. It is based upon a very successful British play and the bulk of the film clearly betrays this origin; there are about three interior locations, and both performers and camera are stock still most of the time. The actors are very wooden by Hollywood standards. The characters are one-dimensional (to put it charitably).

However, "The Wrecker" is well worth watching because this is a railway melodrama and the film opens the stage play way up with lots of very, very impressive location photography of steam trains in motion and, count 'em, three spectacular wrecks done full scale, actual trains, no miniatures. The railway atmosphere (including views of Waterloo Station) is wonderful and the wrecks are jaw-dropping.

"The Wrecker" may not hit the mark of a quality major American film, mostly due to flat directing; but there's no doubt that Gainsborough spent real money and effort by British standards of the time.

The film was released in silent and in synchronized versions with brief talking sequences. The silent version was used here. One of the sound sequences was quite effectively faked.

It appears to me that the film was mastered from a 16mm print made in later years from the somewhat worn camera negative. The tonal range is excellent and there are very few visible splices or jump cuts. Only a slight softness of the image suggests that this does not come from 35mm.Read more ›

5.0 out of 5 starsTrain heaven
By norloll on 24 Dec. 2009
Format: DVD Verified Purchase
Hurrah! This is a rocking silent train thriller from the British Cinema. It was directed by Geza Von Bolvary and is a kind of twin to his version of The Ghost Train (also based on a play by Arnold Ridley). Bolvary was brought over from Europe specially to make this as part of the Film Europe movement, and no expense was spared on the production - the set designs are really entertaining, but of course the set-piece train crashes are the main appeal for many fans. No special effects here, the production company actually took the locomotives to an unused piece of line and wrecked them for real, filming the action with multiple cameras. If you are not familiar with British Silent productions it may take you a little time to get used to the style of the movie, but once you have a world of fabulousness is in store for you. The film features Benita Hume, a luminiously beautiful star of the period.

It's such a delight and a surprise to see this rare film released on DVD and in such a handsome edition - the transfer is excellent, and seems to be from a very well preserved print. There is an excellent piano score, specially composed by Neil Brand, the internationally renowned silent film accompanist (and also railway enthusiast). And there is a veritable cornucopia of extras, including the original trailer, a 9.5mm cut-down version produced for the home movie market, as well as commentaries and documentaries and host of other train-related material. A really lavish range of goodies for the discerning viewer. The package has clearly been put together with steam-train enthusiasts in mind, and it certainly does this market proud.Read more ›
