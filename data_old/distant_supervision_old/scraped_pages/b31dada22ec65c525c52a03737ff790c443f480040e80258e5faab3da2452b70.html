I go back and forth on just how strong Cincinnati's candidacy for MLS is, from sometimes thinking there's a legit shot they could be fast-tracked to 2018 so that an Eastern team can enter with LAFC to convinced there's no way they beat out Detroit, St. Louis, Sacramento and San Diego for a spot.

    It's going to be a fun day, though. I'm taking the day off work (along with a bunch of other members of my SG) to be part of whatever festivities occur throughout the day.
    I think the NASL's biggest mistakes were 1) letting the NY Cosmos tail wag the dog, and 2) getting involved with Traffic Sports USA. Originally, the teams that split from USL to form NASL did so because they didn't like it when Nike sold its ownership stake in USL to a holding company rather than sell it to team owners, which to me was a completely reasonable course of action. Of course most of those owners and many of the original teams have either joined MLS, rejoined USL, or folded, and in that process gave the Cosmos and Traffic way too much influence in how the league was run. I think if it weren't for Traffic and the Cosmos, some of the better teams in the USL would consider moving up to NASL, but with Traffic being taken out by the FIFA scandal and the Cosmos run by a bunch of delusional nut jobs, I can understand why owners would prefer joining a more stable USL that has a friendly relationship with MLS rather than an antagonistic one with past ties to an organization being prosecuted for corruption.

    It should be interesting to see how Garber and MLS approach the FC Cincinnati situation. Last year I would have said "no way, no how" does Cincinnati get into the conversation for an MLS team. There are bigger cities without soccer and Columbus is right up the road. But I don't think anyone could have predicted how wildly successful FC Cincinnati would be in it's first year. When you get to the point where you're garnering attention not just from US-based soccer press, but international media, it puts you in a unique situation where it almost makes it tough for Garber to say no. And the funny thing about having Columbus up the road is that, while FC Cincinnati was setting attendance records and doing well on the field, CCSC had a terribly disappointing season, yet still managed to increase attendance over last year's MLS Cup runner-up. So it could very well be that having an in-state rivalry may be a boon to both FC Cincinnati and CCSC. Who knows. There's still so much up in the air with the next expansion. Right now the only for sure things are Atlanta and Minnesota in 2017.

    I liked this part:
    Current USL teams Sacramento Republic and FC Cincinnati are huge success stories (Sacramento averaged 11,514 for home games and Cincinnati an incredible 17,296) and both have been tabbed to become MLS’ next expansion franchises, with Sacramento already making a major push with their new downtown stadium site.

    With so many USL teams going on to join MLS after building strong bases in the third-tier, is that something Edwards would continue to welcome moving forward?

    “Five of the last seven have done so when you’re talking about Orlando, Seattle, Portland, Vancouver and Montreal. Clubs like that have had time in the USL whether it be a couple of years or 10-15 years, and what they’ve been able to do is build their brand and build a solid club and build a soccer culture in that community which may or may not have been on the radar of Major League Soccer,” Edwards explained. “When you look at markets like Orlando and Cincinnati, who probably weren’t on MLS’ radar, then through the USL they are able to start building professional soccer fandom in that city. If they do that at a high enough level for long enough then it may be something that they may entertain down the road. Sacramento are going through that process right now. I fully anticipate it.
